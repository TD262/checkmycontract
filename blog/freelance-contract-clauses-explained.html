Every Freelance Contract Clause
Explained in Plain English

Indemnification. Governing law. Work for hire. These terms sound intimidating — but they're not complicated once someone explains them clearly. Here's every clause you'll encounter, and exactly what it means for you.

📅 2025 ⏱ 11 min read ✍️ CheckMyContract

You've received a freelance contract. It's full of words like "indemnification," "intellectual property assignment," and "governing law." You're not sure if any of it is a problem — or what half of it even means.

You're not alone. Most freelancers feel this way. Legal language is deliberately precise, which often makes it feel more complicated than it actually is.

This guide breaks down every major clause you'll find in a freelance contract — what it means in plain English, what to watch out for, and what a fair version looks like.

The Clauses You'll Find in Almost Every Freelance Contract

📋
Scope of Work
Also called: Statement of Work, Deliverables, Services
High risk if vague

What it means

This defines exactly what you're being hired to do. It should list every deliverable — what you'll create, in what format, by when, and to what standard. This is the single most important clause in the contract.

⚠️ Watch out for: Vague language like "complete the project as discussed" or "ongoing support as needed." If it's not written down, the client can claim it was always part of the deal.
✓ What good looks like: A detailed list of specific deliverables — page counts, file formats, features, word counts — plus an explicit statement that anything not listed requires a separate agreement.
💰
Payment Terms
Also called: Compensation, Fees, Invoicing
High risk if vague

What it means

This covers how much you'll be paid, when you'll be paid, and how. It should include the total fee or rate, payment schedule (upfront deposit, milestones, or on completion), invoice payment window (e.g. Net-14 means they have 14 days to pay after you invoice), and late payment penalties.

⚠️ Watch out for: "Payment upon satisfactory completion" — this gives the client unlimited power to delay payment by claiming they're not satisfied. Also watch for no deposit required, which means you carry all the financial risk.
✓ What good looks like: 50% deposit before work begins, 50% due within 14 days of delivery. Late payments incur a 1.5% monthly fee.
✏️
Revision Policy
Also called: Revisions, Amendments, Change Requests
High risk if unlimited

What it means

This defines how many rounds of changes are included in your fee, what counts as a revision versus a new request, and what happens if the client wants more changes than the contract allows.

⚠️ Watch out for: "Revisions until client satisfaction" or no revision clause at all. Both mean unlimited unpaid revisions.
✓ What good looks like: Two rounds of consolidated revisions included. Additional revisions billed at $X/hour. New creative directions constitute a new project.
©️
Intellectual Property (IP) Ownership
Also called: Work for Hire, Copyright Assignment, IP Transfer
Critical — read carefully

What it means

By default, you own everything you create. This clause determines when — and if — ownership transfers to the client. "Work for hire" means the client owns it from the moment it's created. "IP assignment upon payment" means you own it until they pay you in full. The timing matters enormously.

⚠️ Watch out for: IP transferring upon delivery rather than upon payment. If the client receives your work and then refuses to pay, you've lost both the payment and your ownership rights.
✓ What good looks like: All intellectual property transfers to Client upon receipt of full and final payment. Until payment is received, Freelancer retains all rights.
"Work for hire" is a specific legal term in US copyright law. If the contract says "this is a work for hire," the client owns the copyright automatically — even your right to be credited as the creator.
🔒
Confidentiality / NDA
Also called: Non-Disclosure Agreement, Confidentiality Agreement
Medium risk

What it means

You agree to keep certain information about the client or project private. This is reasonable — clients have legitimate business secrets. The question is how broad the clause is, how long it lasts, and whether it prevents you from showing the work in your portfolio.

⚠️ Watch out for: Clauses that prevent you from even mentioning the client's name or showing finished work in your portfolio. Your portfolio is your livelihood — losing the right to display your work can directly hurt your future income.
✓ What good looks like: Confidentiality applies to business information, trade secrets, and client data — not to the existence of the project or the finished deliverables. Portfolio usage is explicitly permitted.
🚫
Non-Compete Clause
Also called: Non-Competition, Competitive Restriction
High risk — often unenforceable

What it means

You agree not to work with competitors of the client for a set period of time after the project ends. For employees, this is common. For freelancers, it can be devastating — especially if the restricted industry is your primary market.

⚠️ Watch out for: Broad industry definitions, long durations (anything over 6 months is unusual for freelancers), and geographic scope. A clause saying you can't work with any "digital marketing company" for 2 years could effectively shut down your business.
✓ What good looks like: Either no non-compete at all, or a narrow version: "Freelancer agrees not to directly solicit Client's existing customers for 6 months following project completion."
🤝
Non-Solicitation Clause
Also called: Non-Solicitation Agreement
Medium risk

What it means

You agree not to approach the client's employees, contractors, or customers to offer them your services directly. This is different from a non-compete — it's narrower and generally more reasonable. You can still work in the same industry; you just can't poach their people or clients.

⚠️ Watch out for: Clauses that are so broad they prevent you from working with anyone who has ever interacted with the client's business, or that last longer than 12 months.
✓ What good looks like: Narrow, time-limited (6–12 months), and limited to existing clients and employees — not every company in the same market.

Termination Clause
Also called: Cancellation, Early Termination
High risk if one-sided

What it means

This defines who can end the contract, how much notice they have to give, and what happens to payment when they do. A good termination clause protects you if the client cancels mid-project — through a kill fee (a percentage of the remaining contract value you're owed even if they cancel).

⚠️ Watch out for: Contracts where the client can cancel any time for any reason with no financial obligation, but you're required to give 30 days notice or finish the project first. This asymmetry leaves you completely exposed.
✓ What good looks like: Either party may terminate with 14 days written notice. If Client terminates after work has begun, Client owes Freelancer for all work completed to date plus a kill fee of 25% of the remaining contract value.
⚖️
Indemnification Clause
Also called: Hold Harmless, Indemnity
High risk — often overlooked

What it means

Plain English: if something goes wrong and someone gets sued, this clause determines who pays. An indemnification clause typically says you agree to cover the client's legal costs if a third party sues them over your work. The danger is how broadly it's written — some clauses make you liable for things that are entirely outside your control.

⚠️ Watch out for: One-sided indemnification (you cover them but they don't cover you), and clauses with no dollar cap on your liability. If the clause has no ceiling, you could theoretically be on the hook for millions.
✓ What good looks like: Mutual indemnification (each party covers their own mistakes) plus a liability cap: "In no event shall either party's liability exceed the total fees paid under this agreement."
🏛️
Dispute Resolution
Also called: Arbitration Clause, Mediation
Medium risk

What it means

This defines how disagreements are handled if they can't be resolved informally. Options include going to court, mediation (a neutral third party helps you reach a deal), or arbitration (a neutral third party makes a binding decision). Many contracts require arbitration instead of court — this is generally fine for freelancers, but read the details.

⚠️ Watch out for: Arbitration clauses that require you to use a specific (expensive) arbitration service, waive your right to a class action, or require you to travel to a distant location for proceedings.
✓ What good looks like: Disputes first go to mediation. If unresolved, binding arbitration through a neutral service like JAMS or AAA, conducted remotely.
🗺️
Governing Law & Jurisdiction
Also called: Choice of Law, Venue
Medium risk

What it means

This determines which state's (or country's) laws apply to the contract, and where any legal proceedings would take place. If you're in California and the contract says disputes must be resolved in New York courts, you'd have to file legal action in New York — which is expensive and often impractical.

⚠️ Watch out for: Jurisdiction being set in the client's location when you're in a different state or country. This is often used as a deterrent — making it too expensive for you to pursue a legitimate claim.
✓ What good looks like: Your own state, or a mutual agreement on remote arbitration so location doesn't matter.
🌪️
Force Majeure
Also called: Act of God clause
Low risk — standard

What it means

If something completely outside either party's control — a natural disaster, pandemic, war — prevents the contract from being fulfilled, neither party is held liable for the failure. This is standard in most contracts and generally not a concern for freelancers.

⚠️ Watch out for: Overly broad definitions that allow the client to invoke force majeure for things that aren't truly unforeseeable — like a change in their budget or priorities.
📌
Entire Agreement Clause
Also called: Integration Clause, Merger Clause
Important to understand

What it means

This clause says the written contract is the complete and final agreement between you and the client — and that anything discussed or promised before signing (emails, calls, verbal agreements) is no longer binding. Only what's written in the contract counts.

⚠️ Watch out for: Signing a contract with an entire agreement clause when the client has made verbal promises that aren't in the document. Once you sign, those promises are legally gone.
✓ What to do: Make sure everything the client promised you is actually written into the contract before you sign. Don't rely on "we'll sort that out later."
💡 Bottom line: You don't need to be a lawyer to understand your contract. You just need to know what each clause does and what a fair version looks like. If something feels off — or you're just not sure — that's exactly what CheckMyContract is for.

The Bottom Line

Legal language exists to be precise, not to confuse you. Once you know what each clause does, reading a contract gets much faster and much less stressful.

The clauses that matter most for freelancers are payment terms, IP ownership, revision policy, termination rights, and indemnification. Get those right and you've protected the vast majority of what could go wrong.

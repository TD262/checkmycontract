What Every Freelance Contract
Should Include

A missing clause isn't just an oversight — it's a gap in your protection. Here's the complete checklist of what every freelance contract needs, with example language you can use right now.

📅 2025 ⏱ 8 min read ✍️ CheckMyContract

Most freelancers focus on spotting bad clauses in a contract. That's important — but there's an equally dangerous problem: clauses that are missing entirely.

A contract without a revision limit leaves you open to unlimited unpaid changes. A contract without a kill fee means the client can cancel after you've done half the work and owe you nothing. A contract without a payment window means "I'll pay you eventually" is a legally acceptable answer.

What's not in the contract can hurt you just as much as what is.

This is the complete checklist of what every freelance contract should include — organized by priority, with example language you can reference or use directly.

Essential Clauses — Non-Negotiable

These are the clauses every single freelance contract must have. If any of these are missing, ask for them before you sign.

Essential — must have
Important — strongly recommended
Situational — depends on project
✓

1. Names and Details of Both Parties

Full legal names, business names if applicable, and contact details for both you and the client. This sounds obvious but is often missing from informal contracts.

If missing: The contract may not be enforceable — it needs to clearly identify who made the agreement.
Example language"This Agreement is entered into between [Your Full Name / Business Name] ("Freelancer") and [Client Full Name / Company Name] ("Client"), effective [Date]."
✓

2. Detailed Scope of Work

An explicit list of exactly what you will deliver — formats, quantities, features, pages, word counts, or whatever is relevant to your work. Plus a clear statement that anything not listed is out of scope.

If missing: The client can claim anything they want was "always part of the project." This is the number one cause of scope creep.
Example language"Freelancer will deliver: [specific list of deliverables]. Any work not listed above constitutes a change in scope and requires a written change order and additional compensation."
✓

3. Payment Amount and Schedule

The exact fee, when each payment is due, how to invoice, and what payment methods are accepted. No vague language about "upon completion."

If missing: You have no legal basis to demand payment by a specific date. "Completion" can be delayed indefinitely.
Example language"Total fee: $[amount]. Payment schedule: 50% ($[amount]) due upon signing; 50% ($[amount]) due within 14 days of final delivery. Invoices unpaid after 14 days accrue interest at 1.5% per month."
✓

4. Project Timeline and Deadlines

Start date, key milestones, and final delivery date. Also include what happens if the client is late providing feedback or materials — delays caused by them should not penalize you.

If missing: The client can claim you missed a deadline you never agreed to, or hold you to an unreasonable timeline set verbally.
Example language"Project begins [date]. Final delivery by [date], contingent on Client providing all required materials and feedback within 3 business days of each request. Client delays extend all deadlines proportionally."
✓

5. Revision Policy

How many rounds of revisions are included, what counts as a revision, and what happens when the client wants more than what's included.

If missing: Revisions are unlimited. The client can request changes forever and you have no contractual basis to charge for them.
Example language"Two rounds of consolidated revisions are included in the project fee. Additional revisions will be billed at $[hourly rate]/hour. Requests for new creative directions or significant scope changes are not revisions and will be quoted separately."
✓

6. Intellectual Property Ownership

Who owns the work, and exactly when ownership transfers. This should always be tied to payment — not delivery.

If missing: Ownership defaults to you under copyright law — but if the contract is silent, it creates ambiguity that can lead to disputes.
Example language"Upon receipt of full and final payment, Freelancer assigns all intellectual property rights in the deliverables to Client. Until full payment is received, Freelancer retains all rights and Client may not use the work."
✓

7. Termination and Kill Fee

How either party can end the contract, how much notice is required, and what the client owes if they cancel after work has begun.

If missing: The client can cancel at any time with no financial obligation, even after you've completed significant work.
Example language"Either party may terminate this agreement with 14 days written notice. If Client terminates after work has commenced, Client shall pay for all work completed to date plus a kill fee equal to 25% of the remaining unpaid contract value."

Important Clauses — Strongly Recommended

These clauses aren't always present in basic contracts but can save you significantly when things go wrong.

★

8. Confidentiality (with Portfolio Carve-Out)

Agree to keep the client's business information private — but make sure the clause explicitly allows you to show the finished work in your portfolio and list them as a past client.

If missing or too broad: You may be prohibited from ever mentioning the client or showing the work, which directly hurts your future earnings.
Example language"Freelancer agrees to keep Client's confidential business information private. Notwithstanding the above, Freelancer may display completed deliverables in their portfolio and reference Client as a past client, unless Client requests otherwise in writing within 30 days of project completion."
★

9. Liability Cap

A clause that limits your total financial exposure to the amount the client actually paid you. Without this, your liability is theoretically unlimited.

If missing: If your work causes downstream harm — even indirectly — you could be liable for damages far exceeding your project fee.
Example language"In no event shall Freelancer's total liability to Client exceed the total fees paid by Client under this agreement."
★

10. Independent Contractor Status

A statement that you are an independent contractor, not an employee. This protects both parties from tax and labor law complications.

If missing: Ambiguity around your status can create tax liability issues for both you and the client.
Example language"Freelancer is an independent contractor and not an employee of Client. Freelancer is responsible for all taxes, insurance, and other obligations arising from this engagement."
★

11. Expense Reimbursement

If the project requires you to purchase stock images, software, fonts, or other assets — this clause specifies whether those costs are included in your fee or reimbursed separately.

If missing: You may end up personally absorbing project costs the client expected you to cover, or vice versa.
Example language"Any third-party costs (stock assets, software licenses, printing, etc.) exceeding $50 will be pre-approved by Client and reimbursed within 14 days of invoice."
★

12. Warranties and Representations

You warrant that your work is original, doesn't infringe on anyone else's rights, and that you have the right to enter into this agreement. The client warrants the same about any materials they provide you.

If missing: Unclear responsibility if a third party claims your work infringes their copyright.

Situational Clauses — Include When Relevant

These don't belong in every contract but are important in specific situations.

+

13. Subcontracting Rights

If you might need to bring in other freelancers to help with the project, this clause gives you the right to do so. Important for larger projects or if you run a small studio.

+

14. Approval Process

Defines how deliverables are "accepted" — what the client needs to do to formally approve work, and what happens if they don't respond within a set timeframe (deemed approval).

Example language"Client has 5 business days to review and approve each deliverable. If no written feedback is received within 5 business days, the deliverable is deemed approved."
+

15. Credit and Attribution

For creative work — design, writing, photography — this specifies whether you receive a byline or design credit on the published work. Especially valuable for portfolio-building.

+

16. Rush Fee Policy

If the client requests faster turnaround than the agreed timeline, this clause gives you the right to charge a rush premium — typically 25–50% above your standard rate.

💡 Practical tip: You don't need all 16 of these in every contract. Focus on the 7 essential clauses first — those cover the vast majority of situations where freelancers get hurt. Add the others as your projects get larger and more complex.

What to Do if a Contract Is Missing Key Clauses

If a client sends you a contract that's missing essential clauses, don't just sign and hope for the best. Here's what to do:

For short contracts or simple agreements: Ask the client to add the missing clauses before you sign. Frame it as making sure you're both protected: "I'd like to add a couple of standard clauses to make sure we're both covered — happy to send the suggested language."

For very simple projects with trusted clients: You can supplement a thin contract with a detailed email that outlines payment terms, scope, and revisions — then get the client to reply confirming they agree. This creates a paper trail even if the contract itself is sparse.

For larger or higher-stakes projects: Consider sending your own contract rather than working from theirs. Many experienced freelancers have their own standard contract that includes all the protections they need — and send it first.

⚠️ Never start work without a signed agreement. A handshake deal or email exchange is better than nothing, but a signed contract is the only thing that truly protects you. Even the most trusted clients can have payment issues, go out of business, or dispute what was agreed.

The Bottom Line

A good freelance contract isn't about distrust — it's about clarity. When both parties know exactly what's expected, what's included, and what happens if things go sideways, the project runs more smoothly for everyone.

Use this checklist every time you receive or send a contract. The 7 essential clauses should be non-negotiable. The rest you can add as your situation requires.

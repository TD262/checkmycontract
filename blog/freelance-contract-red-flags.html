🚨 Contract Red Flags

10 Freelance Contract
Red Flags You Should
Never Ignore

Most freelancers sign bad contracts because they don't know what to look for. Here are the 10 warning signs that should make you pause — or walk away — before you put pen to paper.

📅 2025 ⏱ 8 min read ✍️ CheckMyContract

You landed a new client. The project sounds exciting, the rate is good, and they seem professional. Then they send you a contract — and it's 12 pages of dense legal language you barely understand.

Most freelancers do one of two things: they skim it and sign, or they spend hours Googling terms and still aren't sure what they agreed to. Either way, they're at risk.

The truth is, a bad contract can cost you far more than a bad client. It can mean working for free, losing ownership of your work, being sued, or being locked out of your own industry. Knowing what to look for is the single most valuable skill a freelancer can have.

Here are the 10 red flags we see most often — and what to do about each one.

1. Vague Payment Terms

1
No clear payment amount, schedule, or late fees

If the contract doesn't specify exactly how much you'll be paid, when you'll be paid, and what happens if they pay late — you have almost no legal protection when things go wrong. "Payment will be made upon completion" is not good enough. Completion of what? Within how many days? What if they dispute it?

Red flag language: "Payment due upon satisfactory completion of the project."
Ask for: A specific dollar amount, a fixed payment schedule (e.g. 50% upfront, 50% on delivery), a payment window (e.g. "within 14 days of invoice"), and a late fee clause (e.g. 1.5% per month on overdue balances).

2. Unlimited Revisions

2
"Revisions until the client is satisfied"

This is one of the most common ways freelancers end up working for free. If the contract doesn't cap the number of revisions, a client can request changes indefinitely — and you're contractually obligated to keep making them. What started as a $3,000 project can turn into six months of unpaid revision cycles.

Red flag language: "Freelancer will revise work until client's satisfaction is achieved."
Ask for: A specific revision limit (e.g. "Two rounds of revisions are included. Additional revisions will be billed at $X per hour."). Also define what constitutes a "revision" vs. a new request.

3. Vague or Missing Scope of Work

3
The project isn't clearly defined

If the deliverables aren't spelled out in detail, the client can keep expanding what they expect from you — and claim it was always part of the deal. This is called scope creep, and it's one of the biggest sources of freelancer burnout and underpayment. Without a clear scope, you have no defense.

Red flag language: "Freelancer will complete the website project as discussed."
Ask for: A detailed list of specific deliverables — pages, features, formats, word counts, file types — whatever is relevant to your work. Include what is explicitly NOT included. Anything not on the list should require a change order and additional payment.

4. Immediate IP Transfer

4
Ownership transfers before you're paid

By default in most countries, the person who creates a work owns it — until you sign a contract that says otherwise. Many contracts transfer intellectual property (IP) to the client the moment work is delivered, regardless of whether you've been paid. This means they can use your work for free if payment falls through. You have zero leverage.

Red flag language: "All work product created under this agreement shall be the sole property of Client upon delivery."
Ask for: "Intellectual property transfers to Client upon receipt of full payment." This one sentence protects you enormously. Until they pay, you own it.

5. Broad Non-Compete Clauses

5
Restrictions on working with other clients

A non-compete clause prevents you from working with competitors of your client for a specified period. For employees, this is normal. For freelancers, it can be career-ending. If you're a web designer and a client's non-compete bans you from working with any other "digital marketing company" for two years, you may have just accidentally agreed to stop working in your industry.

Red flag language: "Freelancer agrees not to work with any competing business in the same industry for a period of 24 months following the termination of this agreement."
Ask for: Remove it entirely, or narrow it dramatically. A fair version might be: "Freelancer agrees not to directly solicit Client's existing customers for a period of 6 months." Industry-wide bans for a freelancer are almost always unenforceable and unreasonable.

6. One-Sided Termination Rights

6
The client can cancel any time, but you can't

Many contracts allow the client to terminate "at will" — for any reason, at any time — while requiring you to give 30 days notice or finish the project before leaving. This imbalance means you can be cut off from payment overnight while being held to lengthy obligations. If there's no kill fee clause, you may get nothing for work you've already completed.

Red flag language: "Client may terminate this agreement at any time with or without cause. Freelancer may not terminate without 30 days written notice."
Ask for: Mutual termination rights with equal notice periods, and a kill fee — meaning if they cancel after work has begun, they owe you a percentage of the remaining contract value (typically 25–50%).

7. Liability and Indemnification Clauses That Go Too Far

7
You're personally responsible for their losses

Indemnification clauses require you to cover the client's legal costs if something goes wrong. In some contracts, this extends to situations that are completely outside your control — like if their product causes harm and they get sued. Without a liability cap, you could be personally responsible for damages that far exceed your project fee. This is especially risky if you don't have business insurance.

Red flag language: "Freelancer shall indemnify and hold harmless Client from any and all claims, damages, losses, and expenses arising from Freelancer's work."
Ask for: A mutual indemnification clause (you each cover your own mistakes) and a liability cap: "In no event shall either party's liability exceed the total fees paid under this agreement."

8. Unfavorable Governing Law

8
Disputes must be resolved in their state or country

If a contract specifies that any dispute must be resolved in the client's jurisdiction — especially if they're in another state or country — you'd have to file legal claims there. This is often used as a deterrent. Even if you're completely in the right, the cost and hassle of pursuing action in another jurisdiction may make it not worth it. The client knows this.

Red flag language: "This agreement shall be governed by the laws of the State of Delaware, and all disputes shall be resolved exclusively in courts located in Wilmington, Delaware."
Ask for: Your own state and jurisdiction. Or agree to neutral dispute resolution like binding arbitration through a service like JAMS, which allows remote participation.

9. Overly Broad Confidentiality Clauses

9
You can't show the work in your portfolio

Confidentiality clauses protect legitimate business secrets — which is reasonable. But some go further and prohibit you from mentioning the client's name, showing the work in your portfolio, or even listing them as a past client. As a freelancer, your portfolio is your business. Signing away the right to show your work can directly harm your future earning potential.

Red flag language: "Freelancer agrees to keep all aspects of this engagement, including but not limited to the existence of this agreement, strictly confidential."
Ask for: A carve-out: "Freelancer may list Client as a past client and display completed work in their portfolio, unless Client requests otherwise in writing within 30 days of project completion."

10. No Deposit Required

10
The whole payment comes at the end

If you're doing all the work upfront and getting paid only after everything is "approved," you're carrying 100% of the financial risk. Clients can delay approval, disappear, dispute the work, or simply go out of business. Without a deposit, you have no leverage and no protection. This is the number one cause of freelancers getting stiffed.

Red flag language: "Payment of the full project fee shall be due upon final delivery and client approval."
Ask for: A minimum 25–50% deposit before work begins, with the remainder due upon delivery — not upon "approval." Approval is subjective; delivery is not. Some freelancers also use milestone payments for longer projects.
💡 Pro tip: You don't have to push back on every clause. Start with the two or three that affect your money and your ownership rights most directly — payment terms, IP transfer, and revision limits. Clients who push back hard on all three simultaneously may be clients worth walking away from.

What to Do When You Spot a Red Flag

First — don't panic, and don't sign. A client sending you a one-sided contract isn't necessarily acting in bad faith. Many clients reuse the same template they've always used without thinking through how it affects the person on the other side. Simply asking to negotiate specific clauses is normal and professional.

Here's a simple process:

1. List every clause you want changed, specifically. Don't just say "the contract feels unfair." Point to the exact language and what you'd like instead. This keeps the conversation professional and efficient.

2. Propose concrete alternatives. Don't just object — offer a solution. "I'd like to change X to Y" is much easier for a client to respond to than "I'm not comfortable with X."

3. Prioritize your must-haves. Pick your three most important changes and lead with those. You're more likely to get what matters most if you don't treat every clause as a dealbreaker.

4. Get everything in writing. Any change you agree to verbally needs to be reflected in the final signed contract. "We talked about it" is not binding.

5. Walk away if necessary. A client who refuses to negotiate any terms is telling you something important about how they'll treat you throughout the project. The right clients understand that a fair contract protects both parties.

The Bottom Line

Reading and negotiating contracts is a skill. It takes time to develop, and most freelancers learn it the hard way — after getting burned once. The good news is that once you know what to look for, reviewing a contract takes 15–20 minutes and can save you thousands of dollars and months of stress.
